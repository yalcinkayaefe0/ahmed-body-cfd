\section{Introduction}

The Ahmed body~\cite{ahmed1984} is a simplified bluff-body geometry that has become the standard benchmark for automotive aerodynamics CFD validation. Its geometry is fully parametric and its flow field—particularly the slant-angle-dependent wake structure—is extensively documented in the open literature.

This study uses the \textbf{25° slant angle} configuration at $U_\infty = \SI{40}{m/s}$
($Re \approx 2.77\times10^6$). Two distinct experimental sources are used, and it is important not
to conflate them:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Force coefficients} are taken from Meile et al.~\cite{meile2016}, who measured
  $C_d = 0.29883 \pm 0.5\%$ on the original geometry at \SI{40}{m/s} using a six-component force
  balance, and Meile et al.~\cite{meile2011}, who report $C_l = 0.345$.
  \item \textbf{Wake velocity profiles} are taken from Lienhart \& Becker~\cite{lienhart2003}, who
  published LDA measurements in the wake. That work does \emph{not} report force coefficients, and
  is therefore not a valid citation for $C_d$ or $C_l$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Objectives}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform a three-level mesh independence study using the GCI methodology~\cite{celik2008}.
  \item Validate the fine-mesh $C_d$ against the experimental reference.
  \item Establish the experimental wake reference (ERCOFTAC Case 082) as the basis for a subsequent
  profile-by-profile comparison.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mesh Independence Study}

\subsection{GCI Methodology}

For three meshes with cell counts $N_1 > N_2 > N_3$ and quantities $f_1, f_2, f_3$, the grid
refinement ratios and the differences between successive solutions are
\begin{equation}
  r_{21} = \left(\frac{N_1}{N_2}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  r_{32} = \left(\frac{N_2}{N_3}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{21} = f_2 - f_1, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{32} = f_3 - f_2 .
\end{equation}

The apparent order of accuracy $p$ follows from Celik et al.~\cite{celik2008}:
\begin{align}
  p &= \frac{1}{\ln r_{21}}
       \left| \ln\left|\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right| + q(p) \right|, \\
  q(p) &= \ln\!\left( \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} - s}{r_{32}^{\,p} - s} \right), \qquad
  s = \operatorname{sign}\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right).
\end{align}

The term $q(p)$ vanishes only for a uniform refinement ratio ($r_{21} = r_{32}$). In the present
mesh family $r_{21} = 1.742$ and $r_{32} = 1.314$, so $q(p)$ must be retained; since $p$ appears on
both sides of Eq.~(2), the system is solved by fixed-point iteration. The extrapolated value and
the fine-grid convergence index are then
\begin{align}
  f_\text{ext}^{21} &= \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} f_1 - f_2}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1}, \qquad
  e_{21,a} = \left| \frac{f_1 - f_2}{f_1} \right|, \qquad
  \text{GCI}_\text{fine} = \frac{1.25\, e_{21,a}}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1} .
\end{align}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Drag Coefficient Underprediction}
Against the stilt-corrected reference the fine mesh underpredicts $C_d$ by $1.3$–$4.4\%$. The
comparison against the raw measured value ($-9.4\%$) overstates the error, because it charges the
stilt-free CFD geometry with drag that the experimental model's support stilts contributed.

With a fine-mesh $\text{GCI}$ of only $0.77\%$, the \emph{numerical} uncertainty is an order of
magnitude smaller than the remaining deviation. The residual discrepancy is therefore
\textbf{modelling error, not discretisation error}, and is consistent with the literature for
$k$-$\omega$ SST with wall functions on bluff bodies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Wall function buffer region:} $y^+\approx15$–$30$ sits between the viscous sublayer and log-law validity, introducing near-wall velocity gradient errors.
  \item \textbf{RANS separation modelling:} $k$-$\omega$ SST underpredicts the slant recirculation zone at 25°, reducing base pressure drag.
  \item \textbf{Non-monotonic error vs.\ experiment:} Against the raw measured value the coarse mesh
  appears almost exact ($+0.3\%$) while the fine mesh appears worst ($-9.4\%$). This is an artefact
  of compensating errors: excess numerical diffusion on the coarse mesh inflates the predicted drag,
  which happens to offset both the model's underprediction and the missing stilt drag. Agreement
  with experiment on an unconverged mesh is not evidence of a correct solution — the grid
  convergence study is what exposes this.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Lift Coefficient — Sign Error, Resolved}
\label{sec:cl_disc}

An earlier revision of this report carried an inverted $C_l$ on the medium and fine meshes. The
error has since been traced and corrected; it is documented here because the diagnosis is
instructive.

The experimental value for the 25° slant is $C_l = +0.345$~\cite{meile2011} — \emph{positive}.
Gutierrez et al.~\cite{gutierrez2020} obtain $+0.363$ ($k$-$\varepsilon$ Realizable) and $+0.376$
($k$-$\omega$ Standard), and explain the mechanism: at 25° the flow between the road and the
underbody is slow, so the underbody static pressure remains higher than that on the upper surface,
producing net positive lift. The medium and fine runs returned a negative value of the same
magnitude.

That pattern was itself the diagnosis. A physical modelling deficiency cannot negate a coefficient
while preserving its magnitude — it would corrupt the magnitude instead. The fault therefore had to
lie in post-processing, and it did: the lift report definition's force vector had been entered as
$(0,-1,0)$ on the medium and fine cases against $(0,1,0)$ on the coarse case, inverting the vertical
axis. Correcting the vector and re-extracting $C_l$ from the existing converged solutions — no
re-iteration, so the flow fields are untouched — recovers the magnitudes to five significant figures:

Medium (837k) & $-0.3235$ & $\mathbf{+0.32349}$ & $-6.2\%$ \\

\noindent
All three meshes now agree on both sign and magnitude. The residual $-6.3\%$ is the same order as
the $C_d$ deviation.

\textbf{However, this does not on its own constitute a validated $C_l$.} The corrected value is a
moving-ground result compared against a fixed-ground measurement, so the residual cannot yet be
attributed to turbulence modelling — see Sec.~\ref{sec:ground_disc}.

\subsection{Ground Boundary Condition — Open Issue}
\label{sec:ground_disc}

\textbf{The ground condition simulated here does not match either experiment it is compared
against.} This study applies a moving wall at $U = \SI{40}{m/s}$ (Table~1), emulating a rolling
road. Both experimental sources ran over a \emph{stationary} floor:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Ground condition in the reference experiments.}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Source & Facility & Ground \\
\midrule
Lienhart \& Becker~\cite{lienhart2003,ercoftac082} & LSTM, 3/4-open section & Fixed plate; model on
\SI{30}{mm} stilts \\
Meile et al.~\cite{meile2011,meile2016} & Graz ISW & Fixed floor, six-component balance \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
The distinction is not cosmetic. Strachan et al.~\cite{strachan2007} measured this geometry over
both a fixed and a moving ground plane and found that the moving plane thins the underbody boundary
layer, increases the underbody flow speed, and displaces the near-wake vortex structure. Those are
precisely the mechanisms that set the underbody static pressure — and therefore $C_l$, the
coefficient already flagged in Sec.~\ref{sec:cl_disc}.

The consequence is that the lift comparison is doubly compromised: beyond the sign error, the
sign-corrected $+0.323$ is a \emph{moving-ground} CFD result being measured against a
\emph{fixed-ground} experiment. The residual $-6.3\%$ therefore cannot be attributed to turbulence
modelling until the ground condition is matched; part of it is a boundary-condition mismatch. $C_d$
is less sensitive to the ground state than $C_l$, but is not immune either.

\textbf{Action required:} rerun the fine mesh with the ground boundary set to a stationary wall,
restarted from the converged moving-ground solution with no other setting altered, and report both
values. This isolates the ground condition as the single changed variable and makes the resulting
comparison like-for-like.

\subsection{Mesh Independence}
The apparent order $p = 3.53$ exceeds the formal second-order accuracy of the discretisation
scheme. Such super-convergence typically indicates that the mesh family is not perfectly
self-similar: the aspect-ratio-based boundary layer sizing means the prism layers do not refine at
the same rate as the poly-hexcore freestream cells, so $y^+$ is not held constant across mesh
levels. The refinement ratios themselves are also non-uniform ($r_{21}=1.742$ vs.\ $r_{32}=1.314$),
which is precisely why the $q(p)$ term in Eq.~(3) cannot be neglected.

Reporting the GCI at both the iterated $p = 3.53$ ($0.77\%$) and the theoretical $p = 2$ ($2.30\%$)
brackets the numerical uncertainty; in both cases it remains well below the $1.3$–$4.4\%$ modelling
error, so the conclusions are insensitive to the choice. A fixed first-layer thickness and a
uniform refinement ratio would yield a cleaner mesh family and an apparent order closer to 2.

\subsection{Wake Profiles — Withdrawn}
\label{sec:wake_disc}

An earlier revision of this report presented a CFD-vs-experiment wake comparison with an MAE metric.
That comparison has been \textbf{withdrawn rather than corrected}, because neither side of it was
sound:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The experimental reference was not the experiment.} The profiles had been entered
  by hand at stations $x/H = 0.48$, $0.61$, $0.73$, $0.86$. The ERCOFTAC Case 082
  dataset~\cite{ercoftac082} — the actual Lienhart \& Becker measurements — provides planes at
  $x/H = 0$, $0.28$, $0.69$, $1.74$. The stations that were plotted do not exist in the experiment,
  and the values did not match it: the real data shows reverse flow reaching $U/U_\infty = -0.34$ at
  $x = \SI{80}{mm}$, an order of magnitude stronger than what had been tabulated.
  \item \textbf{The CFD profiles have no recorded station.} The Fluent export
  (\texttt{results/fine/velocity\_profiles.xy}) labels its four blocks
  \texttt{wake\_x1}--\texttt{wake\_x4} and records no streamwise coordinate, so the location of the
  computed profiles cannot be recovered from the file.
\end{itemize}

Correcting the reference alone would not have made the comparison valid, since the computed side
remains unlocated. The remedy is to re-export the CFD symmetry-plane profiles at the four
experimental stations and plot them against the ERCOFTAC data directly, which
\texttt{scripts/ercoftac\_wake\_reference.py} is set up to do. This is left as the immediate next
step; no wake validation claim is made in this revision.

\section{Conclusions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Mesh independence is achieved on the fine mesh: $\text{GCI}_\text{fine} = 0.77\%$ ($2.30\%$
  if $p$ is fixed at the theoretical second order), with the asymptotic range check satisfied
  ($0.96$). The Richardson-extrapolated value is $C_d = 0.2682$.
  \item Against a stilt-corrected experimental reference, $C_d = 0.270$ underpredicts by
  $1.3$–$4.4\%$. Since the numerical uncertainty is below $1\%$, this residual is attributable to
  \emph{modelling} error — the known limitations of $k$-$\omega$ SST with wall functions on
  separated bluff-body flow — rather than to insufficient mesh resolution.
  \item Comparing a stilt-free geometry directly against the measured value inflates the apparent
  error to $9.4\%$. Likewise, the coarse mesh appears to agree almost exactly with experiment
  ($+0.3\%$) purely through compensating errors. Both are reminders that a validation claim is only
  as good as the like-for-like nature of the comparison behind it.
  \item A lift sign error introduced between the coarse and medium runs has been traced to the force
  report definition's direction vector, entered as $(0,-1,0)$ instead of $(0,1,0)$, and corrected.
  Re-extracting $C_l$ from the existing converged solutions gives $+0.32349$ (medium) and $+0.32319$
  (fine), recovering the original magnitudes to five significant figures and bringing all three
  meshes into agreement on sign and magnitude (Sec.~\ref{sec:cl_disc}).
  \item The ground boundary condition (moving wall at freestream speed) matches neither reference
  experiment, both of which used a stationary floor. Since the moving ground alters exactly the
  underbody flow that sets the lift, the $C_l$ comparison is not like-for-like and the residual
  deviation cannot yet be assigned to turbulence modelling. A stationary-ground rerun is required
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:ground_disc}).
  \item The wake comparison presented in an earlier revision has been withdrawn: its experimental
  reference did not correspond to the actual measurements, and the computed profiles carry no
  recorded station coordinate (Sec.~\ref{sec:wake_disc}). The genuine ERCOFTAC Case 082 data is now
  included in the repository, and the comparison is the immediate next step rather than a claim made
  here.
\end{itemize}

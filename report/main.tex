\section{Introduction}

The Ahmed body~\cite{ahmed1984} is a simplified bluff-body geometry that has become the standard benchmark for automotive aerodynamics CFD validation. Its geometry is fully parametric and its flow field‚Äîparticularly the slant-angle-dependent wake structure‚Äîis extensively documented in the open literature.

This study uses the \textbf{25¬∞ slant angle} configuration at $U_\infty = \SI{40}{m/s}$
($Re \approx 2.77\times10^6$). Two distinct experimental sources are used, and it is important not
to conflate them:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Force coefficients} are taken from Meile et al.~\cite{meile2016}, who measured
  $C_d = 0.29883 \pm 0.5\%$ on the original geometry at \SI{40}{m/s} using a six-component force
  balance, and Meile et al.~\cite{meile2011}, who report $C_l = 0.345$.
  \item \textbf{Wake velocity profiles} are taken from Lienhart \& Becker~\cite{lienhart2003}, who
  published LDA measurements in the wake. That work does \emph{not} report force coefficients, and
  is therefore not a valid citation for $C_d$ or $C_l$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Objectives}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform a three-level mesh independence study using the GCI methodology~\cite{celik2008}.
  \item Validate the fine-mesh $C_d$ against the experimental reference.
  \item Compare computed wake velocity profiles with Lienhart \& Becker (2003) at four downstream stations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mesh Independence Study}

\subsection{GCI Methodology}

For three meshes with cell counts $N_1 > N_2 > N_3$ and quantities $f_1, f_2, f_3$, the grid
refinement ratios and the differences between successive solutions are
\begin{equation}
  r_{21} = \left(\frac{N_1}{N_2}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  r_{32} = \left(\frac{N_2}{N_3}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{21} = f_2 - f_1, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{32} = f_3 - f_2 .
\end{equation}

The apparent order of accuracy $p$ follows from Celik et al.~\cite{celik2008}:
\begin{align}
  p &= \frac{1}{\ln r_{21}}
       \left| \ln\left|\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right| + q(p) \right|, \\
  q(p) &= \ln\!\left( \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} - s}{r_{32}^{\,p} - s} \right), \qquad
  s = \operatorname{sign}\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right).
\end{align}

The term $q(p)$ vanishes only for a uniform refinement ratio ($r_{21} = r_{32}$). In the present
mesh family $r_{21} = 1.742$ and $r_{32} = 1.314$, so $q(p)$ must be retained; since $p$ appears on
both sides of Eq.~(2), the system is solved by fixed-point iteration. The extrapolated value and
the fine-grid convergence index are then
\begin{align}
  f_\text{ext}^{21} &= \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} f_1 - f_2}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1}, \qquad
  e_{21,a} = \left| \frac{f_1 - f_2}{f_1} \right|, \qquad
  \text{GCI}_\text{fine} = \frac{1.25\, e_{21,a}}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1} .
\end{align}

\textbf{Stilt correction.} The error column above compares a \emph{stilt-free} CFD model against an

Subtracting this from the measured value gives an effective stilt-free reference of
$C_d = 0.2735$‚Äì$0.2822$, against which the present fine-mesh result of $0.2699$ deviates by only
$-1.3\%$ to $-4.4\%$. This is the comparison the geometry actually supports.

\noindent
Because the apparent order $p = 3.53$ exceeds the formal second-order accuracy of the
discretisation scheme, the GCI was recomputed with $p$ fixed at the theoretical value of 2, as
recommended by Celik et al.~\cite{celik2008}. This yields $C_d^\text{ext} = 0.2649$ and
$\text{GCI}_\text{fine} = 2.30\%$ ‚Äî the same conclusion, with the fine-mesh numerical uncertainty
remaining well below the deviation from experiment. The asymptotic range check is satisfied for
$p = 3.53$ ($0.96$) but not for $p = 2$ ($1.68$), indicating the iteratively solved $p$ is the more
self-consistent estimate for this mesh family.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Drag Coefficient Underprediction}
Against the stilt-corrected reference the fine mesh underpredicts $C_d$ by $1.3$‚Äì$4.4\%$. The
comparison against the raw measured value ($-9.4\%$) overstates the error, because it charges the
stilt-free CFD geometry with drag that the experimental model's support stilts contributed.

With a fine-mesh $\text{GCI}$ of only $0.77\%$, the \emph{numerical} uncertainty is an order of
magnitude smaller than the remaining deviation. The residual discrepancy is therefore
\textbf{modelling error, not discretisation error}, and is consistent with the literature for
$k$-$\omega$ SST with wall functions on bluff bodies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Wall function buffer region:} $y^+\approx15$‚Äì$30$ sits between the viscous sublayer and log-law validity, introducing near-wall velocity gradient errors.
  \item \textbf{RANS separation modelling:} $k$-$\omega$ SST underpredicts the slant recirculation zone at 25¬∞, reducing base pressure drag.
  \item \textbf{Non-monotonic error vs.\ experiment:} Against the raw measured value the coarse mesh
  appears almost exact ($+0.3\%$) while the fine mesh appears worst ($-9.4\%$). This is an artefact
  of compensating errors: excess numerical diffusion on the coarse mesh inflates the predicted drag,
  which happens to offset both the model's underprediction and the missing stilt drag. Agreement
  with experiment on an unconverged mesh is not evidence of a correct solution ‚Äî the grid
  convergence study is what exposes this.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Lift Coefficient ‚Äî Open Issue}
\label{sec:cl_disc}

\textbf{This result is not yet validated and should not be used.} The present simulation returns
$C_l = -0.323$, whereas the experimental value for the 25¬∞ slant is $C_l = +0.345$~\cite{meile2011}
‚Äî \emph{positive}. Gutierrez et al.~\cite{gutierrez2020} obtain $+0.363$ ($k$-$\varepsilon$
Realizable) and $+0.376$ ($k$-$\omega$ Standard), and explain the physical mechanism: at 25¬∞ the
flow between the road and the underbody is slow, so the underbody static pressure remains higher
than that on the upper surface, producing net positive lift.

The magnitude of the present result ($0.323$) is within a few percent of the experimental value,
while its sign is opposite. This pattern points to a \textbf{sign or direction-vector error in the
lift force report definition} rather than a physical modelling deficiency: had the discrepancy been
physical, the magnitude would not agree so closely. The lift direction vector in the Fluent force
report must be verified against the geometry's vertical axis before any $C_l$ conclusion is drawn.

No physical interpretation of the negative $C_l$ is offered here, because the most likely
explanation is a post-processing error and any such interpretation would be unfounded.

\subsection{Mesh Independence}
The apparent order $p = 3.53$ exceeds the formal second-order accuracy of the discretisation
scheme. Such super-convergence typically indicates that the mesh family is not perfectly
self-similar: the aspect-ratio-based boundary layer sizing means the prism layers do not refine at
the same rate as the poly-hexcore freestream cells, so $y^+$ is not held constant across mesh
levels. The refinement ratios themselves are also non-uniform ($r_{21}=1.742$ vs.\ $r_{32}=1.314$),
which is precisely why the $q(p)$ term in Eq.~(3) cannot be neglected.

Reporting the GCI at both the iterated $p = 3.53$ ($0.77\%$) and the theoretical $p = 2$ ($2.30\%$)
brackets the numerical uncertainty; in both cases it remains well below the $\approx6\%$ modelling
error, so the conclusions are insensitive to the choice. A fixed first-layer thickness and a
uniform refinement ratio would yield a cleaner mesh family and an apparent order closer to 2.

\subsection{Wake Profiles}

\textbf{Caveat ‚Äî the experimental data used in this comparison is not yet verified.} The reference
profiles in \texttt{scripts/lienhart\_comparison.py} were entered manually as digitised values and
have not been checked against the source dataset (ERCOFTAC Classic Collection, Case C.81). Until
that check is done, the quantitative agreement reported below ‚Äî including the MAE figure ‚Äî must be
treated as provisional.

Subject to that caveat, the wake trend follows the expected shape: a velocity deficit near the
ground recovering towards freestream above body height, with the deficit overpredicted relative to
experiment. Better agreement would require a finer near-wall mesh ($y^+<1$) or a scale-resolving
approach such as LES.

\section{Conclusions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Mesh independence is achieved on the fine mesh: $\text{GCI}_\text{fine} = 0.77\%$ ($2.30\%$
  if $p$ is fixed at the theoretical second order), with the asymptotic range check satisfied
  ($0.96$). The Richardson-extrapolated value is $C_d = 0.2682$.
  \item Against a stilt-corrected experimental reference, $C_d = 0.270$ underpredicts by
  $1.3$‚Äì$4.4\%$. Since the numerical uncertainty is below $1\%$, this residual is attributable to
  \emph{modelling} error ‚Äî the known limitations of $k$-$\omega$ SST with wall functions on
  separated bluff-body flow ‚Äî rather than to insufficient mesh resolution.
  \item Comparing a stilt-free geometry directly against the measured value inflates the apparent
  error to $9.4\%$. Likewise, the coarse mesh appears to agree almost exactly with experiment
  ($+0.3\%$) purely through compensating errors. Both are reminders that a validation claim is only
  as good as the like-for-like nature of the comparison behind it.
  \item \textbf{Open items.} The computed $C_l$ has the wrong sign and is not yet validated
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:cl_disc}); the digitised wake reference data has not been checked against the
  ERCOFTAC source dataset. Neither result should be cited until resolved.
\end{itemize}

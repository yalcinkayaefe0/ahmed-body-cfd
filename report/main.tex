\section{Introduction}

The Ahmed body~\cite{ahmed1984} is a simplified bluff-body geometry that has become the standard benchmark for automotive aerodynamics CFD validation. Its geometry is fully parametric and its flow field—particularly the slant-angle-dependent wake structure—is extensively documented in the open literature.

This study uses the \textbf{25° slant angle} configuration at $U_\infty = \SI{40}{m/s}$
($Re_L = 2.78\times10^6$ on the body length, equivalently $Re_H = 7.68\times10^5$ on the body
height). Reynolds numbers throughout this report use the kinematic viscosity adopted by the
reference experiment, $\nu = \SI{15e-6}{m^2/s}$~\cite{ercoftac082}, which reproduces the $Re_H$
quoted there exactly. Two distinct experimental sources are used, and it is important not to
conflate them:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Force coefficients} are taken from Meile et al.~\cite{meile2016}, who measured
  $C_d = 0.29883 \pm 0.5\%$ on the original geometry at \SI{40}{m/s} using a six-component force
  balance, and Meile et al.~\cite{meile2011}, who report $C_l = 0.345$.
  \item \textbf{Wake velocity profiles} are taken from Lienhart \& Becker~\cite{lienhart2003}, who
  published LDA measurements in the wake. That work does \emph{not} report force coefficients, and
  is therefore not a valid citation for $C_d$ or $C_l$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Objectives and Scope}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Perform a three-level mesh independence study using the GCI methodology~\cite{celik2008}.
  \item Validate the fine-mesh $C_d$ and $C_l$ against the experimental references, ensuring the
  comparison is like-for-like in both geometry (support stilts) and boundary conditions (ground
  motion).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The scope is deliberately limited to the \textbf{integrated force coefficients}. Validation of the
resolved wake structure against the LDA profiles is a separate claim and is not made here; the
experimental dataset is included in the repository for subsequent work, and the reasons are set out
in Sec.~\ref{sec:wake_disc}.

\noindent
\textbf{The maximum aspect ratio is effectively identical at all three levels, despite a twelvefold
increase in cell count.} This is a direct measurement of how the mesh family was constructed: the
boundary-layer specification (8 layers, first aspect ratio 40, growth rate 1.2) was held fixed at
every level, so refinement acted on the hexcore freestream cells alone while the prism stack
remained unchanged.

This bears directly on the mesh independence study. Section~\ref{sec:mesh_disc} attributes the
apparent order $p = 3.53$ — in excess of the scheme's formal second-order accuracy — to a mesh
family that is not self-similar, with prism and freestream cells refining at different rates and
$y^+$ consequently not held constant across levels. Table~\ref{tab:mesh_quality} supplies the
quantitative evidence for that argument, which had until now rested on reasoning alone. Specifying
a fixed first-layer \emph{thickness} rather than a fixed aspect ratio would refine both regions
together and yield a cleaner family.

\begin{sloppypar}
\textbf{Location of the worst cells.} All three meshes place them at the same position, all within
zone 308: $(0.506,\,0.0499,\,0.178)$ for coarse, $(0.522,\,0.0586,\,0.186)$ for medium and
$(0.518,\,0.0500,\,0.190)$ for fine. Interpreted against the geometry,
$y \approx \SI{0.050}{m}$ is precisely the ground clearance, $x \approx \SI{0.51}{m}$ is mid-body
($L = \SI{1.044}{m}$) and $z \approx \SI{0.18}{m}$ lies near the side edge (half-width
\SI{0.194}{m}). The worst cells therefore sit at the \emph{lower side edge of the underbody}, where
the prism stack is compressed into a sharp dihedral corner. In each mesh the worst
orthogonal-quality cell is simultaneously the worst-skewness cell, with the worst-aspect-ratio cell
less than a millimetre away. Refinement does not relocate them, the constraint being geometric
rather than resolution-driven. Given $\text{GCI}_\text{fine} = 0.77\%$, their effect on the solution
is not measurable.
\end{sloppypar}

A maximum aspect ratio of $\approx122$ against a target first-layer aspect ratio of 40 is expected:
the target governs the first layer only, whereas the reported maximum is taken over the entire
boundary-layer stack.

\section{Mesh Independence Study}
\label{sec:mesh_independence}

\subsection{GCI Methodology}

For three meshes with cell counts $N_1 > N_2 > N_3$ and quantities $f_1, f_2, f_3$, the grid
refinement ratios and the differences between successive solutions are
\begin{equation}
  r_{21} = \left(\frac{N_1}{N_2}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  r_{32} = \left(\frac{N_2}{N_3}\right)^{1/3}, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{21} = f_2 - f_1, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{32} = f_3 - f_2 .
\end{equation}

The apparent order of accuracy $p$ follows from Celik et al.~\cite{celik2008}:
\begin{align}
  p &= \frac{1}{\ln r_{21}}
       \left| \ln\left|\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right| + q(p) \right|, \\
  q(p) &= \ln\!\left( \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} - s}{r_{32}^{\,p} - s} \right), \qquad
  s = \operatorname{sign}\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_{32}}{\varepsilon_{21}}\right).
\end{align}

The term $q(p)$ vanishes only for a uniform refinement ratio ($r_{21} = r_{32}$). In the present
mesh family $r_{21} = 1.742$ and $r_{32} = 1.314$, so $q(p)$ must be retained; since $p$ appears on
both sides of Eq.~(2), the system is solved by fixed-point iteration. The extrapolated value and
the fine-grid convergence index are then
\begin{align}
  f_\text{ext}^{21} &= \frac{r_{21}^{\,p} f_1 - f_2}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1}, \qquad
  e_{21,a} = \left| \frac{f_1 - f_2}{f_1} \right|, \qquad
  \text{GCI}_\text{fine} = \frac{1.25\, e_{21,a}}{r_{21}^{\,p} - 1} .
\end{align}

Subtracting this from the measured value gives an effective stilt-free reference of

\noindent
Because the apparent order $p = 3.53$ exceeds the formal second-order accuracy of the
discretisation scheme, the GCI was recomputed with $p$ fixed at the theoretical value of 2, as

remaining well below the deviation from experiment. The asymptotic range check is satisfied for

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Drag Coefficient Underprediction}
\label{sec:cd_disc}
Against the stilt-corrected reference the fine mesh underpredicts $C_d$ by $1.3$–$4.4\%$. The
comparison against the raw measured value ($-9.4\%$) overstates the error, because it charges the
stilt-free CFD geometry with drag that the experimental model's support stilts contributed.

With a fine-mesh $\text{GCI}$ of only $0.77\%$, the \emph{numerical} uncertainty is an order of
magnitude smaller than the remaining deviation. The residual discrepancy is therefore
\textbf{modelling error, not discretisation error}, and is consistent with the literature for
$k$-$\omega$ SST with wall functions on bluff bodies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Wall function buffer region:} $y^+\approx15$–$30$ sits between the viscous sublayer and log-law validity, introducing near-wall velocity gradient errors.
  \item \textbf{RANS separation modelling:} $k$-$\omega$ SST underpredicts the slant recirculation zone at 25°, reducing base pressure drag.
  \item \textbf{Non-monotonic error vs.\ experiment:} Against the raw measured value the coarse mesh
  appears almost exact ($+0.3\%$) while the fine mesh appears worst ($-9.4\%$). This is an artefact
  of compensating errors: excess numerical diffusion on the coarse mesh inflates the predicted drag,
  which happens to offset both the model's underprediction and the missing stilt drag. Agreement
  with experiment on an unconverged mesh is not evidence of a correct solution — the grid
  convergence study is what exposes this.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Lift Coefficient — Sign Error, Resolved}
\label{sec:cl_disc}

An earlier revision of this report carried an inverted $C_l$ on the medium and fine meshes. The
error has since been traced and corrected; it is documented here because the diagnosis is
instructive.

The experimental value for the 25° slant is $C_l = +0.345$~\cite{meile2011} — \emph{positive}.
Gutierrez et al.~\cite{gutierrez2020} obtain $+0.363$ ($k$-$\varepsilon$ Realizable) and $+0.376$
($k$-$\omega$ Standard), and explain the mechanism: at 25° the flow between the road and the
underbody is slow, so the underbody static pressure remains higher than that on the upper surface,
producing net positive lift. The medium and fine runs returned a negative value of the same
magnitude.

That pattern was itself the diagnosis. A physical modelling deficiency cannot negate a coefficient
while preserving its magnitude — it would corrupt the magnitude instead. The fault therefore had to
lie in post-processing, and it did: the lift report definition's force vector had been entered as
$(0,-1,0)$ on the medium and fine cases against $(0,1,0)$ on the coarse case, inverting the vertical
axis. Correcting the vector and re-extracting $C_l$ from the existing converged solutions — no
re-iteration, so the flow fields are untouched — recovers the magnitudes to five significant figures:

\textbf{However, this does not on its own constitute a validated $C_l$.} The corrected value is a
moving-ground result compared against a fixed-ground measurement, so the residual cannot yet be
attributed to turbulence modelling — see Sec.~\ref{sec:ground_disc}.

\subsection{Ground Boundary Condition — Mismatch Identified and Corrected}
\label{sec:ground_disc}

\textbf{The ground condition originally simulated did not match either experiment it was compared
against.} The initial configuration applied a moving wall at $U = \SI{40}{m/s}$, emulating a rolling
road. Both experimental sources ran over a \emph{stationary} floor:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Ground condition in the reference experiments.}
\begin{tabular}{lll}

\noindent
The distinction is not cosmetic. Strachan et al.~\cite{strachan2007} measured this geometry over
both a fixed and a moving ground plane and found that the moving plane thins the underbody boundary
layer, increases the underbody flow speed, and displaces the near-wake vortex structure. Those are
precisely the mechanisms that set the underbody static pressure — and therefore $C_l$, the
coefficient already flagged in Sec.~\ref{sec:cl_disc}.

\subsubsection*{Corrected configuration}

The fine mesh was therefore rerun with the ground boundary set to a stationary wall, restarted from
the converged moving-ground solution with \textbf{no other setting altered}. The ground condition is
thus the single isolated variable, and the comparison becomes like-for-like. The solution
reconverged within 70--80 iterations to residuals of order $10^{-6}$.

\noindent
Matching the ground condition improves both coefficients. $C_l$ moves from $-6.3\%$ to $+3.0\%$ of
the measured value — less than half the deviation, and now marginally above rather than below
experiment. It also coincides with $+0.355$, the value reported by an independent stationary-ground
reference computation for this geometry. $C_d$ rises into the stilt-corrected experimental band
rather than sitting below it.

The relative magnitudes are themselves evidence for the mechanism. $C_l$ changes by $9.9\%$ against
only $3.4\%$ for $C_d$: lift is roughly three times more sensitive to the ground state than drag.
This is precisely what Strachan et al.~\cite{strachan2007} would predict, since the underbody
pressure field their moving plane perturbs acts principally on the \emph{vertical} force.

The wider methodological point is that a substantial share of what could readily have been
attributed to ``$k$-$\omega$ SST limitations'' was in fact a boundary-condition mismatch. With the
ground condition matched and the stilt geometry difference accounted for, the fine mesh reproduces
the experiment to $+3.0\%$ in $C_l$ and to within the reference band in $C_d$ — without any change
to the turbulence model or its coefficients.

\textbf{Scope of the correction.} Only the fine mesh was rerun on a stationary ground; the mesh
independence study was performed on the moving-ground configuration. Since the GCI is a property of
the spatial discretisation and no mesh parameter was altered, it is expected to carry over, but this
has not been demonstrated and is left as a caveat rather than a claim.

\subsection{Mesh Independence}
\label{sec:mesh_disc}
The apparent order $p = 3.53$ exceeds the formal second-order accuracy of the discretisation
scheme. Such super-convergence typically indicates that the mesh family is not perfectly
self-similar: the aspect-ratio-based boundary layer sizing means the prism layers do not refine at
the same rate as the poly-hexcore freestream cells, so $y^+$ is not held constant across mesh
levels. The refinement ratios themselves are also non-uniform ($r_{21}=1.742$ vs.\ $r_{32}=1.314$),
which is precisely why the $q(p)$ term in Eq.~(3) cannot be neglected.

Reporting the GCI at both the iterated $p = 3.53$ ($0.77\%$) and the theoretical $p = 2$ ($2.30\%$)
brackets the numerical uncertainty; in both cases it remains well below the $1.3$–$4.4\%$ modelling
error, so the conclusions are insensitive to the choice. A fixed first-layer thickness and a
uniform refinement ratio would yield a cleaner mesh family and an apparent order closer to 2.

\subsection{Wake Profiles — Withdrawn and Out of Scope}
\label{sec:wake_disc}

An earlier revision of this report presented a CFD-vs-experiment wake comparison with an MAE metric.
That comparison has been \textbf{withdrawn rather than corrected}, because neither side of it was
sound:

\begin{sloppypar}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The experimental reference was not the experiment.} The profiles had been entered
  by hand at stations $x/H = 0.48$, $0.61$, $0.73$, $0.86$. The ERCOFTAC Case 082
  dataset~\cite{ercoftac082} — the actual Lienhart \& Becker measurements — provides planes at
  $x/H = 0$, $0.28$, $0.69$, $1.74$. The stations that were plotted do not exist in the experiment,
  and the values did not match it: the real data shows reverse flow reaching $U/U_\infty = -0.34$ at
  $x = \SI{80}{mm}$, an order of magnitude stronger than what had been tabulated.
  \item \textbf{The CFD profiles have no recorded station.} The Fluent export file
  \texttt{velocity\_profiles.xy} labels its four blocks \texttt{wake\_x1} through
  \texttt{wake\_x4} and records no streamwise coordinate, so the location of the computed profiles
  cannot be recovered from the file.
\end{itemize}
\end{sloppypar}

Correcting the reference alone would not have made the comparison valid, since the computed side
remains unlocated.

\textbf{Wake validation is therefore outside the scope of this study}, which validates the
\emph{force coefficients} against measurement. The two are distinct claims: $C_d$ and $C_l$ test the
integrated surface loads, whereas wake profiles test the resolved flow structure. Only the former is
claimed here.

The experimental data is nonetheless included in the repository in unmodified form
(\texttt{data/ercoftac/}), together with \texttt{scripts/ercoftac\_wake\_reference.py}, which reads
and plots it. What a subsequent study would need to add is a re-export of the CFD symmetry-plane
profiles at the four measured stations, with careful attention to the coordinate mapping: the
ERCOFTAC origin lies at the \emph{rear face} of the body, which does not coincide with the origin of
the Fluent case. Mismapping those axes is what invalidated the withdrawn comparison in the first
place.

\section{Conclusions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Mesh independence is achieved on the fine mesh: $\text{GCI}_\text{fine} = 0.77\%$ ($2.30\%$
  if $p$ is fixed at the theoretical second order), with the asymptotic range check satisfied
  ($0.96$). The Richardson-extrapolated value is $C_d = 0.2682$.
  \item In the final configuration — stationary ground, matching the experiments — the fine mesh
  gives $C_d = 0.27908$, inside the stilt-corrected experimental band ($0.2735$–$0.2822$), and
  $C_l = +0.35525$, within $+3.0\%$ of the measured $+0.345$. Since the numerical uncertainty is
  below $1\%$, these residuals are physical rather than a resolution artefact.
  \item Comparing a stilt-free geometry directly against the measured value inflates the apparent
  error to $9.4\%$. Likewise, the coarse mesh appears to agree almost exactly with experiment
  ($+0.3\%$) purely through compensating errors. Both are reminders that a validation claim is only
  as good as the like-for-like nature of the comparison behind it.
  \item A lift sign error introduced between the coarse and medium runs has been traced to the force
  report definition's direction vector, entered as $(0,-1,0)$ instead of $(0,1,0)$, and corrected.
  Re-extracting $C_l$ from the existing converged solutions gives $+0.32349$ (medium) and $+0.32319$
  (fine), recovering the original magnitudes to five significant figures and bringing all three
  meshes into agreement on sign and magnitude (Sec.~\ref{sec:cl_disc}).
  \item The ground boundary condition had been specified as a moving wall at freestream speed, while
  both reference experiments used a stationary floor. Rerunning the fine mesh with a stationary
  ground — the only altered setting — moved $C_l$ from $-6.3\%$ to $+3.0\%$ of the measured value
  and lifted $C_d$ into the stilt-corrected band. Lift proved about three times more sensitive to
  the ground state than drag ($9.9\%$ against $3.4\%$), consistent with the underbody-pressure
  mechanism reported by Strachan et al.~\cite{strachan2007}. A material share of the deviation that
  might otherwise have been ascribed to turbulence modelling was therefore a boundary-condition
  mismatch (Sec.~\ref{sec:ground_disc}).
  \item The wake comparison presented in an earlier revision has been withdrawn: its experimental
  reference did not correspond to the actual measurements, and the computed profiles carry no
  recorded station coordinate (Sec.~\ref{sec:wake_disc}). Wake validation is accordingly outside the
  scope of this study, which validates the integrated force coefficients rather than the resolved
  wake structure. The genuine ERCOFTAC Case 082 data is included in the repository for any
  subsequent work.
\end{itemize}
